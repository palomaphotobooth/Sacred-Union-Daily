CHAPTER 15

Awareness of God's Presence

Awareness of God’s Presence in Everyday Life Many people imagine the spiritual life as something that happens only during special moments. They think of prayer as something done during a quiet morning devotion. Worship may be associated with a church gathering. Reading scripture may be seen as a daily spiritual discipline. While these practices are valuable, the New Testament suggests that the presence of God is not limited to specific times or places. Instead, God's presence can be experienced throughout the ordinary rhythms of daily life. The Apostle Paul once wrote a short but powerful instruction to believers: “Pray without ceasing.” At first glance, this instruction may seem impossible. How can anyone pray continuously while also working, caring for family, and fulfilling daily responsibilities? Paul was not suggesting that believers should constantly repeat spoken prayers every moment of the day. Rather, he was pointing toward something deeper: a posture of ongoing awareness of God. Prayer, in this sense, becomes less about specific words and more about a continual openness to God's presence. The spiritual life begins to expand beyond designated moments of devotion and becomes woven into the entire fabric of life. This idea has appeared throughout

Christian history in the writings of many spiritual teachers. One well-known example comes from a humble monk known as Brother Lawrence, who lived in a monastery in the seventeenth century. Brother Lawrence wrote about what he called the practice of the presence of God. He explained that he had learned to experience God's presence not only during formal prayer but also during ordinary activities such as cooking, cleaning, and working in the monastery kitchen. For him, every task became an opportunity to remember that God was near. This awareness did not require dramatic experiences or emotional intensity. It was simply the gentle habit of turning one's attention toward God throughout the day. Brother Lawrence once wrote that even the smallest act done with love and awareness of God could become a form of prayer. This perspective reveals an important truth about the spiritual life. God is not present only in extraordinary moments. He is present in ordinary moments as well. Many contemplative traditions emphasize that the challenge is not bringing God into daily life. The challenge is learning to notice that God is already there. The busyness of modern life often makes this difficult. When people rush from one task to another, their attention becomes fragmented. The mind jumps quickly between responsibilities, worries, and distractions. In such a fast-paced environment, it is easy to forget the quiet presence of God. For this reason, developing awareness often begins with small intentional practices. One simple practice is to pause briefly throughout the day and take a slow breath while remembering that God is present. This moment may last only a few seconds, yet it gently redirects attention toward the spiritual dimension of life. Another helpful practice is gratitude. When people intentionally notice small blessings—such as a kind conversation, a moment of beauty in nature, or the completion of a task—they become more aware of God's goodness in daily life. Gratitude shifts attention away from constant worry and toward recognition of God's provision. Over time, these small moments of awareness begin to accumulate. Life gradually feels less fragmented. The sense of separation between spiritual activities and ordinary activities begins to fade. Work, rest, conversation, and even simple routines can become moments of connection with God. This does not mean that every moment will feel spiritually intense or emotionally uplifting. Often awareness of God's presence is subtle and quiet. It may appear as a gentle sense of peace. It may arise as a moment of clarity or insight. Sometimes it simply feels like the reassurance that one is not alone. These quiet experiences are often more important than dramatic spiritual moments. They form the steady foundation of a life lived in relationship with God. Over time, awareness of God's presence can reshape the way believers approach challenges and difficulties. When problems arise, the first instinct may shift from anxiety toward prayer. Instead of feeling overwhelmed, believers remember that God is present even in moments of uncertainty. This awareness does not remove life's challenges, but it changes how those challenges are faced. Faith becomes less about trying to control every outcome and more about trusting that God remains present through every circumstance. The spiritual life becomes less about reaching special experiences and more about recognizing the sacredness already present in everyday life. This perspective reflects the heart of contemplative Christianity. The goal is not escape from the world. The goal is discovering the presence of God within the world. In the next chapter, we will explore how the practice of gratitude can deepen this awareness even further. Gratitude has long been recognized as one of the most powerful ways to cultivate joy, peace, and awareness of God's goodness in daily life.

Reflection & Practice

Take a moment to pause wherever you are. Take a slow breath. Gently remind yourself of this simple truth: God is present here. You do not need to say anything or do anything special. Simply notice the moment. Over the course of the day, try pausing three or four times to remember this awareness. These brief moments of attention can gradually deepen your sense of connection with God.
