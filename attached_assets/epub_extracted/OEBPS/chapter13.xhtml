PART III — CONTEMPLATIVE CHRISTIANITY

CHAPTER 13

Learning the Practice of Stillness

In the modern world, life moves quickly. Most people spend their days surrounded by constant activity, noise, and information. Phones buzz with notifications, schedules fill with obligations, and the mind rarely has an opportunity to slow down. While this pace of life can make many things possible, it also creates a challenge for the spiritual life. When the mind is constantly occupied, it becomes difficult to notice the quiet movements of the heart or the gentle presence of God. For this reason, many Christian traditions have long emphasized the importance of stillness. Stillness is not merely the absence of activity. It is a posture of the heart that allows a person to become attentive

to God's presence. Throughout the Bible, moments of stillness appear as places where people encounter God in meaningful ways. The prophet Elijah experienced this during a time of great exhaustion and discouragement. According to the story, Elijah waited for God to speak through dramatic events—a powerful wind, an earthquake, and a fire. Yet the voice of God did not appear in those displays. Instead, God spoke through what the text describes as a gentle whisper. This moment reveals something important about how God often communicates. Divine presence is not always found in dramatic experiences. Sometimes it is recognized most clearly in quiet attentiveness. This insight led many Christians throughout history to develop practices that intentionally create space for stillness. One of the simplest of these practices is silent prayer. Silent prayer differs from the kind of prayer many people learn as children. Instead of speaking many words or presenting long requests, silent prayer focuses on simply resting in awareness of God. The purpose is not to empty the mind completely, but to gently quiet the constant stream of thoughts that often dominate attention. In this quiet space, believers learn to listen rather than to speak. This posture of listening reflects an important shift in the spiritual life. Many people approach prayer primarily as a time to communicate their needs and concerns to God. While there is certainly a place for this kind of prayer, contemplative traditions remind us that prayer is also a relationship. Like any relationship, communication flows in both directions. Stillness allows space for the heart to become more attentive to God's presence. At first, practicing stillness can feel unfamiliar. Many people discover that their minds continue racing with thoughts, memories, and worries. This is normal. The human mind naturally moves from one idea to another. The goal of contemplative stillness is not to force the mind into perfect silence. Instead, it is to gently return attention to the awareness of God whenever the mind becomes distracted. Over time, this practice becomes easier. As the mind learns to slow down, the heart becomes more sensitive to subtle experiences of peace and presence. Many believers discover that even a few minutes of quiet reflection can bring a surprising sense of calm. This calm is not merely psychological relaxation. For many people, it feels like a reminder that God is already near. Throughout the centuries, Christian monks and spiritual teachers developed many variations of contemplative prayer. Some traditions encouraged repeating a short phrase from scripture as a way of focusing attention. Others emphasized simply resting in silence. What these practices share in common is the desire to cultivate awareness of God's presence rather than constantly striving to reach Him. This shift reflects the themes we explored earlier in this study. If the Gospel reveals that God has already reconciled humanity through Christ and that the Spirit dwells within believers, then the spiritual life becomes less about searching for God and more about noticing His presence. Stillness becomes a doorway into that awareness. In quiet moments, the heart begins to recognize that God is not distant. The constant noise of the world fades for a moment, and the soul remembers that it is not alone. This realization often brings a deep sense of peace. The pressure to control every outcome begins to loosen. Trust begins to grow. Stillness also creates space for gratitude. When people pause long enough to notice the goodness present in their lives, the heart naturally responds with thankfulness. Gratitude has long been recognized as one of the most powerful practices for deepening spiritual awareness. It shifts attention away from anxiety and toward the recognition of God's provision and care. Over time, the practice of stillness can reshape the way believers experience their daily lives. Moments of silence no longer feel empty. They become opportunities to reconnect with the presence of God. Even ordinary activities—walking, working, or sitting quietly—can become moments of awareness. The spiritual life gradually expands beyond formal times of prayer and begins to influence the entire rhythm of daily living. This does not mean that dramatic spiritual experiences occur every time a person practices stillness. Often the practice feels simple and quiet. Yet even in its simplicity, stillness slowly shapes the heart. It trains believers to recognize that God's presence is not limited to special moments. It is available in every moment. In the next chapter, we will explore another contemplative practice that has helped Christians engage deeply with scripture for centuries. This practice is known as Lectio Divina, a form of sacred reading that invites believers to encounter God's word not only with the mind but also with the heart.

Reflection & Contemplation

Find a quiet place where you can sit comfortably for a few minutes. Close your eyes and take several slow breaths. As you breathe, gently bring your attention to the idea that God is present with you. You may wish to quietly repeat a simple phrase from scripture, such as:

“Be still and know that I am God.” — Psalm 46:10 If your mind begins to wander, simply notice the distraction and gently return your attention to the awareness of God's presence. Spend a few minutes resting in this quiet awareness. When you finish, take a moment to notice how you feel. Stillness often reveals more than we expect.
