PART I — THE INVITATION

CHAPTER 1

The Problem Religion Tried to Solve

From the earliest days of human history, people have sensed that there is something greater than themselves. Across cultures and civilizations, humans have built temples, written sacred texts, created rituals, and developed systems of belief to try to understand the divine. Whether through ancient mythologies, philosophical traditions, or organized religions, the underlying desire has often been the same: humanity has been trying to understand its relationship with God. At the heart of most religious systems lies a common assumption: there is a distance between humanity and God, and that distance must somehow be overcome. Religion, in many ways, developed as humanity’s attempt to bridge that gap. Some traditions attempted to reach God through moral discipline. Others through ritual sacrifices. Some through prayer, meditation, or strict obedience to religious law. Each of these approaches assumed that the divine existed somewhere beyond humanity, and that the role of religion was to help people find a way back to God. This assumption has deeply shaped how many people understand spirituality today. Many believers grow up thinking of God as distant, watching from above, evaluating human behavior, and deciding whether individuals have done enough to earn His approval. In this framework, the spiritual life becomes a constant effort to improve oneself in order to become acceptable to God. The result can be exhausting. People may find themselves continually asking questions like: Am I praying enough? Am I faithful enough? Does God approve of me? What must I do to become closer to Him? These questions reveal something important. Beneath them lies a quiet fear that God might still be far away. This feeling of separation can influence the way people approach almost every aspect of their spiritual lives. Prayer becomes an attempt to reach heaven. Worship becomes a momentary encounter rather than a continual reality. Faith becomes a struggle to believe strongly enough to overcome the perceived distance between the human and the divine. But when we begin reading the New Testament carefully, we start to notice something surprising. The message of Jesus does not seem to focus primarily on teaching people how to reach God. Instead, it announces that God

has already come near. In fact, the opening lines of the Gospel of John declare something extraordinary: "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us." This single statement changes the entire framework of how we understand the relationship between God and humanity. Rather than remaining distant, God entered human existence fully. He did not simply send instructions or moral teachings from heaven. He came personally into the world. The Incarnation reveals something profound about God's nature. God is not distant. He is relational. Throughout the ministry of Jesus, we see a consistent pattern. He spends time with ordinary people. He eats with them, speaks with them, heals them, and welcomes them into friendship. His message repeatedly emphasizes that God's kingdom is not something far away in the future, but something present and unfolding among people. At one point, Jesus even tells his followers: "The kingdom of God is within you." This statement challenges the deeply ingrained belief that God's presence exists only in distant spiritual realms. Instead, it suggests that the divine life may be much closer than people realize. Yet despite these teachings, the sense of separation between humanity and God has continued to shape much of religious thought throughout history. Many people still imagine God as existing primarily in heaven while humanity remains on earth, striving to reach Him through effort and devotion. In this mindset, the Christian life becomes a continual attempt to climb upward toward God. But the New Testament presents a different picture. The Apostle Paul writes extensively about themes such as reconciliation, union, and participation in the life of Christ. Again and again he speaks about believers being “in Christ” and Christ living “in them.” These phrases are not merely poetic expressions. They reflect a deeper theological reality that the early church believed was central to the Gospel. The message of Christianity was not simply that people could learn about God or obey God more effectively. It was that through Jesus Christ, humanity had been reconciled to God. Reconciliation is an important word. It means the restoration of relationship. In the ancient world, reconciliation was used to describe the ending of hostility between two parties. When reconciliation occurred, the barrier that once separated them was removed. According to the New Testament, this is precisely what Christ accomplished. Through His life, death, and resurrection, Jesus removed the barriers that humanity believed separated them from God. The result was not merely forgiveness of sins, but the restoration of relationship between God and humanity. The Apostle Paul describes this in striking language: "God was in Christ reconciling the world to Himself." Notice the direction of the action. God was not waiting for humanity to reconcile themselves to Him. Instead, God Himself took the initiative. This idea represents a dramatic shift in perspective. If reconciliation has already been accomplished through Christ, then the central challenge of the spiritual life may not be trying to reach God after all. Instead, it may be learning to recognize and live within a relationship that has already been restored. This shift in understanding can profoundly change how a person experiences faith. Rather than striving to reach a distant God, the believer begins to explore what it means to live in awareness of God's presence. Rather than approaching prayer as an attempt to get God's attention, prayer becomes a conversation within an already existing relationship. Rather than viewing worship as the rare moment when God shows up, worship becomes the natural response to a presence that has never left. This perspective does not eliminate the importance of spiritual practices such as prayer, scripture reading, or worship. Instead, it changes the motivation behind them. Practices are no longer attempts to earn closeness with God. They become ways of becoming more aware of a closeness that already exists. Throughout Christian history, many mystics have described this realization as a turning point in their spiritual journey. Instead of continually striving to reach God, they began to discover that God had been present all along. This discovery often brought a profound sense of peace. The spiritual life became less about anxious effort and more about learning to rest in God's presence. This does not mean that growth stops or that spiritual maturity becomes unimportant. On the contrary, the journey of faith continues to deepen. But the foundation shifts from fear and striving to trust and relationship. The Gospel begins to feel less like a heavy obligation and more like an invitation. An invitation to discover what it means to live in communion with God. An invitation to experience divine joy. An invitation to explore the possibility that the presence of God may be closer than we ever imagined. This realization will guide the rest of our study. Before we can explore contemplative practices, mystical theology, or the experience of divine joy, we must first understand the foundation of the Gospel itself. The life revealed in Christ begins not with human effort, but with divine initiative. The story of Christianity is not primarily about humanity reaching God. It is about God reaching humanity first.

Reflection & Contemplation

Take a few moments of quiet reflection. Consider the following questions: When you think about God, do you imagine Him as distant or near? Have your spiritual practices ever felt like attempts to earn closeness with God? What would change in your life if you believed that God's presence was already with you? Sit quietly for a few minutes and slowly reflect on the following passage: "Christ in you, the hope of glory." — Colossians 1:27 Breathe slowly and allow the words to settle. What might it mean if this statement were not merely symbolic, but deeply real?
