PART II — THE LIFE OF GOD

CHAPTER 7

God as Relationship

When many people think about God, they often imagine a powerful being who rules over the universe. In this picture, God is primarily understood as a supreme authority—someone who created the world, established moral laws, and governs the course of history. While these ideas contain elements of truth, they only capture part of the Christian understanding of God. The New Testament reveals something far more relational about God’s nature. At the center of Christian theology is the belief that God exists as Trinity: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. For many people, the doctrine of the Trinity can feel confusing or abstract. It is sometimes presented in complicated theological language that can make it seem distant from everyday spiritual life. But when we step back and look at the meaning behind the doctrine, we discover something beautiful. The Trinity reveals that relationship is not something God created later. Relationship exists within the very life of God Himself. Before the universe existed, before time began, God was already living in relationship. The Father, Son, and Holy Spirit exist in a dynamic communion of love, unity, and shared life. This means that love is not something God learned after creating the world. Love is woven into the very fabric of God's nature. The New Testament captures this idea in a simple phrase: “God is love.” This statement is not merely describing one of God's attributes. It is describing the nature of His being. Because God exists in relational unity as Father, Son, and Spirit, love flows eternally within the life of God. This understanding has profound implications for how we think about creation. If God already existed in perfect relationship, then creation was not necessary in order to complete God. God did not create the world because He was lonely or lacking something. Instead, creation can be understood as an expression of divine generosity. The life of love that exists within God overflowed outward into creation. Humanity was created not simply as servants or subjects of God, but as beings capable of participating in that relationship. This is why the Bible often describes humanity as being made in the image of God. To bear God's image means that human beings were designed with the capacity for relationship—with one another and with God Himself. The story of the Gospel reveals that through Christ, this relational purpose is restored. Jesus frequently spoke about His relationship with the Father. In the Gospel of John, He describes a deep unity between Himself and the Father, explaining that everything He does flows from that relationship. He then extends an invitation to His followers that is almost breathtaking in its scope. He prays that believers may experience the same kind of unity: "That they may be one, just as we are one." This prayer suggests that the life shared between the Father and the Son is not meant to remain distant from humanity. Through Christ and the presence of the Holy Spirit, believers are invited to participate in that relationship. This idea forms the foundation for what many theologians describe as participation in the life of God. Instead of viewing the Christian life primarily

as obedience to an external authority, it can also be understood as entering into the relational life of the Trinity. This does not mean that human beings become divine in the same way that God is divine. Rather, it means that through Christ, believers share in the life, love, and fellowship that flows within God. The Christian life becomes relational rather than merely transactional. Instead of focusing solely on rules and obligations, the focus shifts toward communion and participation. Prayer becomes a conversation within the life of God. Worship becomes a joyful response to the love that already surrounds us. Faith becomes the process of learning to trust the relationship that God has opened to humanity through Christ. For many believers, discovering this relational dimension of the Trinity brings a sense of warmth and freedom to their spiritual lives. God is no longer imagined as a distant ruler who must be appeased. He is recognized as a loving Father who invites His children into fellowship. The Spirit becomes the presence of God guiding and comforting believers in everyday life. Christ becomes the bridge through which humanity enters the divine relationship. This understanding helps explain why the New Testament repeatedly speaks about believers being adopted as children of God. Adoption is a relational term. It emphasizes belonging rather than performance. A child does not earn membership in a family through achievements. The child belongs because of the relationship itself. In the same way, believers are invited into God's family through grace. This relational foundation also prepares us to understand a beautiful concept developed by early Christian theologians—an idea that describes the life of the Trinity as a kind of shared movement of love. They called it perichoresis. In the next chapter, we will explore this idea more deeply. It has often been described as the divine dance, a way of understanding how the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit live in perfect unity while sharing their life with the world. Understanding this idea opens the door to one of the most beautiful aspects of Christian mysticism: the possibility that humanity is invited not merely to observe God's life, but to participate in it.

Reflection & Contemplation

Take a few moments to reflect on the words of the Apostle John: "God is love." — 1 John 4:8 Consider these questions: What does it mean for love to exist within the nature of God? How might your view of God change if you understood Him primarily in relational terms? What would it feel like to approach the spiritual life as participation in a relationship rather than as a list of religious duties? Sit quietly for a few moments. Imagine the possibility that the love shared within the life of God is not distant from you. It is something you are invited to experience.
